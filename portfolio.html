Cover Letters

A cover letter is an accompanying letter that serves as the introduction to your resume. No resume should be sent without one. The cover letter is individualized for each position for which you are applying.

Key Elements of a Cover Letter:

  • Professional header with your contact information
  • Personalized salutation addressing the hiring manager
  • Opening paragraph expressing interest in the position
  • Body paragraphs highlighting relevant qualifications
  • Closing paragraph with a call to action
  • Professional sign-off

Cover Letter Example

Dear Hiring Manager,

I am writing to express my interest in the [Position] at [Company]. With my background in [Field], I am confident I possess the skills necessary to excel in this role.

My experience includes [Brief summary of relevant experience].

I look forward to discussing how my qualifications can benefit your team.

Sincerely,
Your Name

6. Research Writing

Mastering Research Writing

Research writing is a fundamental skill in academic and professional environments. This module focused on developing our ability to conduct thorough research, analyze information critically, and present findings in a clear, structured, and academically rigorous manner.

Research Writing Process

1

Topic Selection

Identifying a relevant, focused research question or problem statement that contributes to existing knowledge.

2

Literature Review

Comprehensive examination of existing research and scholarly publications related to the topic.

3

Methodology

Designing appropriate research methods and approaches to address the research question.

4

Data Collection

Gathering relevant data through experiments, surveys, interviews, or other research methods.

5

Analysis

Systematically examining and interpreting collected data to draw meaningful conclusions.

6

Writing and Presentation

Structuring findings into a coherent, well-organized research paper with proper citations.

Key Components of a Research Paper

Abstract

A concise summary of the entire research paper, highlighting purpose, methods, results, and conclusions.

Introduction

Sets the context, states the research problem, and outlines the paper's objectives and structure.

Literature Review

Critical analysis of existing research relevant to the topic, identifying gaps and establishing context.

Methodology

Detailed description of research design, methods, procedures, and tools used in the study.

Results

Presentation of research findings without interpretation, often using tables, graphs, and statistics.

Discussion

Interpretation of results, comparison with existing literature, and implications of findings.

Abstract Writing

An abstract is a concise, standalone summary of your entire research paper, thesis, or presentation that highlights the key aspects of your work to help readers quickly understand its purpose, methodology, findings, and implications.

Four Cs of Abstract Writing

Purpose of an Abstract

To Inform

Provide a brief overview of the entire work, allowing readers to grasp its core message and significance.

To Guide

Help readers decide if the full paper is relevant to their interests and warrants a full read.

To Promote

Encourage readers to engage with your research and understand its contribution to the field.

Key Learnings from this Chapter:

  • Understanding the systematic research writing process from topic selection to publication
  • Mastering the structure and components of academic research papers
  • Developing skills in literature review and critical analysis
  • Learning to write effective abstracts that accurately represent research
  • Applying proper citation methods and academic integrity standards

I completed a research project on "The Impact of Emotional Intelligence on Team Performance," applying the skills learned in this chapter. This experience strengthened my ability to conduct rigorous academic research and present findings in a professional manner.

10. Professional, Telephone and Dining Etiquette

Mastering Professional Etiquette

Professional etiquette encompasses the behaviors and manners expected in business settings, including telephone communication and formal dining. Mastering these skills demonstrates respect, professionalism, and cultural awareness.

Telephone Etiquette

The telephone is one of the most important and commonly used tools in business. Multitudes of businesses, companies, and departments use telephones in their work every day. Unfortunately, sometimes we don't pay attention or make a conscious effort to monitor what kind of message we are sending to our callers and the outside world.

Basic Rules of Telephone Etiquette

Speak Clearly

Speak clearly into the mouthpiece or headset

Avoid Eating

Avoid eating or chewing gum while on a call

Proper Interruption Handling

If interrupted, politely ask the caller to hold and press the HOLD button

Never Lay Receiver Down

Never lay the receiver on the desk without placing the caller on hold

Always Be Courteous

Maintain politeness and professionalism throughout the call

Proper Greeting Guidelines

Larger Organizations
"Thank you for calling (dept. name). How may I direct your call?"
Smaller Organizations
"Thank you for calling (dept. name). May I help you?"
Departments
"(dept. name), Mary Smith," OR "Mary Smith, may I help you?"

Responses to Avoid

"He isn't in yet"
"She's out for coffee"
"He's gone for the day" (and it's 3:00 p.m.)
"She's in, but she's busy"

Properly Concluding Conversations

1
Talk in Past Tense

Use past tense to indicate resolution

2
Use Closing Phrases

"I'm really glad you called" or "I'm glad we resolved this concern"

3
State Action to be Taken

Clearly outline next steps

4
Assert Follow-up Actions

Provide time frames or deadlines
